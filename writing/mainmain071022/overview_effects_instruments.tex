\section{Overview effects of tauf}

\begin{itemize}
	\item as energy becomes more expensive (and price of non-energy good falls as more labor gets available), research gets directed to energy sector \ar potential advantage of taul by keeping research in non-energy sector\\ With knowledge spillovers this effect is muted. Research remains in non-energy sector because it is so backwards. And over time, non-energy catches up despite adverse price effect. 
	\item non-equal capital shares mute the supply side effect of a reduction of fossil demand on the supply of green; instead, supply of non-energy increases \ar even stronger reduction in non-energy price \ar more reseach in direction of energy. 
	\item with knowledge spillovers, green production is higher BAU but also: fossil production surges as green sector catches up. \ar higher tauf to meet emissions. Hence, knowledge spillovers mute the percentage change induced by tauf but also keep net emissions high in absolute terms. Knowledge spillovers mute effect of tauf on emissions because the backward (1) fossil production lower in bau, but also higher level under tauf
	\item kn spil effect on endogenous growth: endogenous growth intensifies the rise in emissions due to knowledge spillovers. (Counters argument in AABH); despite knowledge spillovers tauf has a reducing effect on non-energy research. 
\end{itemize}

\section{Overview effects taul}

\begin{itemize}
	\item heterogeneous capital shares do not introduce compositional effect of taul
	\item with heterogeneous skills, hetero capital shares do now shape the compositional effect of taul since labor input goods are no longer heterogeneous. 
	\item compare effect of taul on emissions and growth to effect of tauf
	\item what is the effect of taul on sn? Sn should rise when labor becomes more expensive and hence non-energy goods
	\item compositional effect of taul on GFF is small (if not negligible) and reduces with endogenous growth. \ar why does endogenous growth not intensify reduction in GFF?
	\item research in levels does not react to lower labor supply, the effect is absorbed by a smaller wage rate which researchers accept 
\end{itemize}
